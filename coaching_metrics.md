1. Bar Path
The bar should travel in a perfectly vertical line when viewed from the side. Any deviation tells a story:

Forward drift on descent → you're losing upper back tightness or the bar is positioned too high, shifting load forward over the toes
Forward drift out of the hole → classic "good morning squat" — hips shooting back faster than the chest rises, meaning your posterior chain is dominating but your quads are losing the fight
Backward drift → rare, but means you're driving the hips through too aggressively before the chest follows
I want to see the bar travel within ~1cm of vertical across the entire rep. I use a plumb line overlay in review. Anything more than 2–3cm of horizontal travel is a technical fault I'll flag


2. Hip vs. Knee Break
This is the first movement cue and it sets the entire rep up:

I want to see a simultaneous hip and knee break — hips hinging back while knees track out, not one before the other
Hips breaking first (more than ~1 frame ahead on 60fps footage): you're turning it into a Romanian deadlift at the top, losing quad tension early, and the hole will be ugly
Knees breaking first: you're squatting like a leg press, torso stays too upright early, then dumps forward at depth
I watch for knee travel: your knees should track over the 2nd–3rd toe consistently. Caving on descent (valgus) is a red flag — but late valgus (only in the hole) vs early valgus (from the start) have completely different fixes


3. Balance / Pressure
I'm watching for where your weight is distributed through the whole rep:

At setup, I want to see your whole foot in contact — heel, ball, and pinky-toe side
Heel rise (any amount) on descent = too much forward lean, or ankle mobility issue
Toe rise = sitting too far back, losing the quad drive
I'll cue you to think 3 points of contact and watch if your tibial angle stays consistent throughout the descent — a sudden shin going more vertical means you dumped your hips back to compensate for something breaking down
From the front, I check if your femurs track symmetrically — a left/right imbalance often means a hip mobility asymmetry or a weak glute med on one side


4. Back Angle and Rigidity
Angle:

For a low-bar squat I expect roughly 45–60° of forward lean depending on your anthropometry (longer femurs = more lean, that's fine)
The back angle should be set within the first 1–2 inches of descent and held constant to the hole. If it changes mid-descent, something is breaking
Coming out of the hole, chest and hips should rise at the same rate — if the hips rise faster, the angle increases and you're grinding a good morning

Rigidity:

I'm watching for spinal flexion under load — specifically thoracic rounding (upper back caving) vs lumbar flexion (butt wink)
A small amount of posterior pelvic tilt at depth (true butt wink) is normal and often unavoidable at competition depth — I'm looking for excessive or dynamic butt wink (meaning it happens suddenly mid-descent, which creates a shear force event)
On heavy sets I watch if the upper back rounds progressively rep-to-rep — that's a fatigue signature and tells me your upper back work is underdeveloped or your bracing is failing


5. Speed and Tempo
I break the rep into 4 phases:

Descent: I want controlled but not slow — typically 2–3 seconds to depth. Too fast = losing tightness and getting bounced around; too slow = bleeding strength and building fatigue
The hole / reversal: I want to see a true stretch-reflex reversal — meaning you're not pausing and grinding, you're catching the bounce with tension already loaded. The moment of reversal should look like a sharp direction change, not a collapse-and-grind
Initial drive (0–25% of ascent): This is where I look hardest. The first 2–3 inches out of the hole are the most diagnostic. Slow, grinding movement here = weak quads, poor positioning, or both
Lockout: Should be confident and fast. If you're decelerating before lockout, your bracing collapsed mid-rep or you lost mental intent


6. Velocity Consistency and Drop

Mean concentric velocity (MCV) on a competition opener should be ~0.30–0.40 m/s. Second attempt ~0.22–0.30. Third attempt can dip to ~0.18 m/s minimum — below that, grinders become misses
Velocity drop across a set: On a work set, I allow up to 10–15% velocity drop before I consider the set done for quality purposes. More than 20% drop = you're training failure, not strength
Velocity consistency rep-to-rep: Each rep in a set should be within ~0.03–0.05 m/s of the last. A sudden drop of 0.08+ m/s on one rep means something broke technically on that rep — I'll rewind and find it
Sticking point velocity: I watch where the bar decelerates most sharply during the ascent. The sticking point (typically 1/4 to 1/3 of the way up) is the most diagnostic spot — if you lose more than 30–40% of your peak velocity there, your rate of force development needs work
